Our Methodology

How we calculate every number — and the science behind it.

Every number VitalMetric produces is backed by peer-reviewed research and established medical formulas. We don't invent numbers — we implement published science. Here's exactly what we use, how it works, and where it comes from.

🧬 Metabolic Age Calculator

Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)

We calculate BMR using the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, published in 1990 and widely considered the most accurate predictive BMR equation for the general population — more accurate than the older Harris-Benedict equation. It estimates the calories your body burns at complete rest.

Male: BMR = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age(y) + 5 Female: BMR = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age(y) − 161

Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)

TDEE is your BMR multiplied by an activity factor, using standard multipliers from the dietary reference intake guidelines:

  • Sedentary (desk job, little exercise) → ×1.2
  • Light (1–3 days/week) → ×1.375
  • Moderate (3–5 days/week) → ×1.55
  • Active (6–7 days/week) → ×1.725
  • Extreme (athlete, physical job) → ×1.9

Metabolic Age

Your metabolic age is determined by comparing your calculated BMR to population average BMR values across age groups, derived from the NHANES (National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey) conducted by the CDC. We find the age bracket whose average BMR is closest to yours. A higher-than-average BMR produces a younger metabolic age; a lower BMR produces an older one.

Body Mass Index (BMI)

BMI is weight in kilograms divided by the square of height in meters (kg/m²). The formula was devised by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 1830s and remains the most widely used population-level weight classification tool. We use the WHO international classification for categories:

BMI = weight(kg) / height(m)² Underweight: <18.5 Normal: 18.5–24.9 Overweight: 25–29.9 Obese I: 30–34.9 Obese II: 35–39.9 Obese III: ≥40

Body Fat Percentage — US Navy Method

The US Navy developed this circumference-based method in 1984 at the Naval Health Research Center. It estimates body fat percentage using neck, waist, and (for women) hip measurements combined with height. While not as precise as DEXA or hydrostatic weighing, it correlates well with those methods and is widely used because it requires only a measuring tape.

Male: BF% = 86.010 × log₁₀(waist − neck) − 70.041 × log₁₀(height) + 36.76 Female: BF% = 163.205 × log₁₀(waist + hip − neck) − 97.684 × log₁₀(height) − 78.387 All measurements in inches.

Body Roundness Index (BRI)

Published in 2013 by Thomas et al., BRI is a newer geometric measure that combines waist circumference and height to estimate body shape. Unlike BMI (which only uses weight and height), BRI accounts for where fat is distributed. A BRI of 1–3 is considered healthy. Higher values indicate a rounder body shape associated with increased visceral fat.

BRI = 364.2 − 365.5 × √(1 − (WC/2π)² / (0.5×H)²) WC = waist circumference (m), H = height (m)

Waist-to-Height Ratio (WHtR)

The "keep your waist to less than half your height" rule emerged from a 2010 systematic review and 2012 meta-analysis led by Dr. Margaret Ashwell. WHtR has been shown to be a better predictor of cardiometabolic risk than BMI alone. A ratio below 0.5 is considered healthy.

WHtR = waist / height (same units for both) <0.4: Low 0.4–0.49: Healthy 0.5–0.59: Elevated risk ≥0.6: High risk

🔬 Accuracy & Limitations

All our calculators use validated, published formulas — but they are estimates, not clinical measurements. Here's what you should know:

  • BMR formulas are accurate within ~10% for most people. They can be less accurate for very muscular or very obese individuals.
  • Navy body fat correlates with DEXA at r ≈ 0.85–0.90, but individual error can be ±3–5%.
  • BRI and WHtR are population-level screening tools — they indicate risk patterns, not diagnoses.
  • Metabolic age compares your BMR to population averages. A "younger" metabolic age doesn't guarantee better health — it's one data point among many.

For medical decisions, always consult a healthcare professional. These calculators are for educational and informational purposes only.
